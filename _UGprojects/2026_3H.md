**Undergraduate Project 2025-26 (3H)** 


<br>

<div style="text-align: justify" markdown="1">

Many systems in nature evolve in time: planets orbit stars, populations grow and decline, financial markets fluctuate, and fluids move through the atmosphere and oceans. These processes are often described mathematically using **ordinary differential equations (ODEs)**.

Since exact solutions are rarely available, **numerical time integration** has become an essential tool for understanding and predicting time-dependent systems across science and engineering. Applications range from climate and weather prediction to epidemiology, neuroscience, finance, and spacecraft dynamics.

The goal of this project is to explore how computers approximate solutions of differential equations and how the quality of these approximations can be analysed mathematically.

</div>

<div style="text-align: justify" markdown="1">

<br>

### What is this project about?

This project introduces the mathematical and computational foundations of **numerical methods for solving ordinary differential equations**.

Topics may include:
- basic time-stepping methods (Euler method, midpoint method)
- higher-order methods such as Runge–Kutta schemes
- local and global errors
- convergence and consistency
- numerical stability
- efficiency and computational cost

One of the central questions is:

&nbsp;

**How can we design numerical methods that are both accurate and stable while remaining computationally efficient?**

&nbsp;

The project can also explore why some numerical methods work well for certain problems but fail dramatically for others.

</div>

<div style="text-align: justify" markdown="1">

<br>

### Possible directions

Depending on your interests, the project may involve:
- deriving and analysing numerical schemes mathematically
- implementing time integration methods in Python, Julia, or MATLAB
- comparing methods on model problems from physics or biology
- investigating stiffness and stability restrictions
- studying how modern high-order methods are constructed

The balance between theory and computation can be adapted to suit the student’s background and interests.

</div>

<div style="text-align: justify" markdown="1">

<br>

This project is suitable for students with an interest in applied mathematics, scientific computing, or mathematical modelling. Familiarity with differential equations and basic programming will be helpful.

</div>
